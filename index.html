Australia Energy & Carbon Dioxide Emissions

This data visualization aims to present the structure of clean energy and carbon dioxide emissions in Australia, targeting the general public.

Overview

Energy Generation vs CO2 Emissions

This scatter plot shows the correlation between the total energy generation (in GWh) and energy-related carbon dioxide emissions (in Mt) of each state. Each point represents a state and is labeled with its abbreviation.

Although there is little data, some positive correlation can still be observed. It is worth noting that Victoria's power generation is higher than that of Western Australia, but due to a higher proportion of clean energy, its carbon dioxide emissions are lower.

Australia Become Greener

This map shows the changing trends of the proportion (%) of renewable energy generation in each state of Australia over time. Use the slider to select different years, and the map will be dynamically updated to reflect the proportion of renewable energy generation in the selected year.

When you slide the slider forward, a notable trend emerges: over the past decade, the depth of green in many regions has significantly increased, vividly illustrating the rapid development of renewable energy in Australia.

Let's Explore The Energy Structure of Australia

Australia's State Energy Structure Breakdown Gourp By Renewable, Hydro And Non-Renewable Energy (Note: Data Missing from 2000-2010)

Non-renewable Energy

Black coal has long occupied a core position in power generation and is the most important source of electricity; Brown coal, Natural gas, also contribute a large amount of electricity, but the dominance of black coal is the most prominent.

Renewable Energy

Such as wind energy, large-scale/small-scale solar photovoltaic, biogas has expanded significantly from the later period, reflecting that the proportion of renewable energy power generation in Australia is continuing to grow, and the trend of energy transformation is gradually emerging. The proportion of electricity generated by hydropower (Hydro) is relatively stable and is a stable component of the clean energy system.

More Close To State

Australia's State Energy Structure Breakdown

  • New South Wales (NSW): Traditional fossil fuels (such as coal) were once the mainstay of power generation, but the proportion of renewable energy has expanded significantly in the later period, showing a notable trend of clean energy replacement.
  • Northern Territory (NT): The overall scale of power generation is small, the energy structure is relatively stable, and it relies on a few core energy types.
  • Queensland (QLD): Coal-based energy has long dominated power generation, but the growth momentum of renewable energy is also gradually emerging.
  • South Australia (SA): The proportion of renewable energy is significantly higher, making it a more leading region in Australia's clean energy transition, with a relatively lower proportion of traditional fossil fuels.
  • Tasmania (TAS): Clean energy such as hydropower dominates absolutely, with a minimal proportion of traditional fossil fuels. It is one of the states most dependent on clean energy.
  • Victoria (VIC): The proportion of traditional coal-based energy is gradually decreasing, while the share of renewable energy continues to rise, showing prominent characteristics of energy structure transformation.
  • Western Australia (WA): It was previously dominated by fossil energy. In recent years, renewable energy has grown rapidly, promoting the development of the energy structure towards diversification and low carbonization.

Back To CO2 Emissions

Coal is the main source of carbon emissions.

This parallel coordinate plot shows the relationship between different energy sources and CO₂ emissions. Each axis represents an non-renewable energy, and the lines connect the data points for each state.

The higher the line is on the "Brown" and "Black Coal" axis, the higher its end point tends to be on the "CO₂ Emissions" axis. This directly proves that brown and black coal are the core energy sources driving carbon emissions. In contrast, the lines corresponding to petroleum products and natural gas are generally lower, making their contribution to carbon emissions relatively limited.

It's not just the energy sector that generates carbon emissions.

The core source of emissions is the energy sector, which has always accounted for the largest proportion and is a key area driving carbon emissions. Industrial Processes, Waste, Agriculture and other sectors also contribute a certain scale of carbon emissions.

The green areas related to Land Use, Land - Use Change and Forestry showed negative values in the later period, which means that this field has transformed from a "carbon emission source" to a "carbon sink" (that is, the amount of carbon dioxide absorbed exceeds the amount emitted).
